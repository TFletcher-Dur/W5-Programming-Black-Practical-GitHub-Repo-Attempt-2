A Brief History Of The World Wide Web

How the World Wide Web came to be a framework for global interactions, Nov 4, 2025

Image of Berners-Lee's original NeXT Web Browser and HyperText Editor from the CERN Website.

According to its creator Tim Berners-Lee, the World Wide Web - or in other words, the method which the majority of people use to access web pages and Internet communication services to this day - was originally created as a "universal linked information system". 1 Developed at CERN, it was initially intended mainly as a means for researchers to share information across the world, much like the Internet itself, 2 3 however was also intended to be an open standard for all to use rather than a proprietary system owned by a small number of organisations. 2

The world's first Web Browser was (for obvious reasons) the one used by the CERN researchers themselves, designed to run on the powerful NeXT computers available at the CERN laboratories and in some other research settings (as one might expect of a system designed primarily for research purposes) and used at the time mainly to access important information such as guides on how to operate the computers and phonebooks for CERN and various other research institutions. NeXT computers being powerful machines, few individual users had access to them, hence the early World Wide Web was somewhat naturally restricted to being an information network accessed almost exclusively by researchers. 2

Soon, however, Nicola Pellow - a student on a placement at the laboratory who would ultimately go on to join the team full time and co-develop the first web browser for the classic Mac OS - would develop the first "Line Mode Browser", which displayed web pages in a text-only format and was developed in the by then relatively (although not fully) standardised C programming language and widely adapted such that it could be used on almost every platform available at the time. This brought the World Wide Web to a larger audience than ever before, with it then being able to run (albeit in a rather user-unfriendly manner) on the home computers possessed by many families across the world. 2

The release of the Line Mode Browser and its relatively widespread use by independent users soon made it clear to Berners-Lee and the others at CERN that the project of developing and expanding the World Wide Web was no longer one which they could achieve alone. As such, in keeping with the open source nature of the project as a whole, Berners-Lee sent out a plea to the other early users of the Internet via the homepage for the entire World Wide Web project and the first ever public Web Page at https://info.cern.ch/hypertext/WWW/TheProject.html (the page being maintained at the same address as always to this day due to its status as an important part of the history of the World Wide Web, a system which has had such a profound impact on our society since its conception) asking them to develop new, better browsers with which to access the World Wide Web. Many users of the web would respond to this plea, writing user-friendly browsers for various different platforms, the existence of these browsers rendering the World Wide Web accessible to an ever growing number of ever more casual users. 2

In 1993, the National Center for Supercomputing Applications (NCSA) at the University of Illinois released its Mosaic Browser for various platforms and CERN published the original WWW browser as an open-source, royalty-free program, and by the end of 1994, thanks to these systems among others, the World Wide Web had over 10 million users and over 10 thousand servers, 20 percent of which were even run commercially rather than academically. From there, as is clear to tell from the very fact that you are reading this page via the World Wide Web in some regard now, the World Wide Web would only continue to grow, connecting together more and more people and fulfilling its role of allowing for the sharing of information - both academic and otherwise - across the world. 2

To give some statistics, the World Wide Web comprises over 1,119,023,272 websites 4 (this being the estimated number of sites as of the 28th of May 2025), with 3 new websites being created every second on average. 5 Across the world, an estimated 6.04 billion people use the Internet, this being around 73.2% of the global population. 6 As such, it is almost undeniable to say that the Internet has spread rapidly and had a drastic and permanent impact on society since its conception, as well as that the World Wide Web remains true to its goal of being an open standard and framework for communication to this day in its status as a highly accessible means for users across the world to communicate with each other, its accessibility being exemplified by the over 5.66 billion social media accounts across the world. 6

The Internet As A Conduit For Cultural and Subcultural Exchanges

With time, the Internet would expand across the world, connecting people of various different cultures speaking various different languages, who inevitably interacted via a variety of means, creating websites such as the WordReference Forums which allow for the exchange of cultural and linguistic knowledge, among other, less serious forums such as Reddit.

The Internet has also, almost since its conception, facilitated subcultural exchanges via forums such as Reddit (among other locations) which have connected together various communities and allowed them to grow, as discussed in the context of the LGBTQ+ community in Japan by Erica Friedman 7 in her book "By Your Side".
